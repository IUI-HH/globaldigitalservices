Industry

The film industry is also finding a role for generative AI in offices and board rooms, where it can be used to automate time-consuming tasks, predict ratings, and save production costs.

Uses

During pre-production phases, AI can be used to provide inspiration for everything from visual themes to filming locations. Tools like Largo.ai can be used to analyze scripts, characters, and actor appearances to make casting recommendations based on commercial potential. Both text and film tools can also be used to create pitch-decks and trailers.

Generative AI tools like Cinelytic can be used to predict the success of films within different demographics, information that can be used to guide acquisition and marketing decisions. During filming, AI can be used to facilitate the creation of complex scheduling grids. Productions can also save money by using AI to create virtual environments that will eliminate the need for expensive--and potentially dangerous--physical sets.

Concerns

A January 2024 report from the consulting firm CVL Economics (based on a survey of 300 entertainment industry leaders) predicts that 62,000 industry jobs will be consolidated, replaced, or eliminated by AI within the next three years. These jobs will likely include staff positions as well as crew and actors. Entry-level positions will be most at risk, since employees in those positions are typically tasked with doing the time-consuming grunt-work that AI can now automate.

In addition to ethical concerns about what data is used by AI scouting and casting models and where it comes from, use of these models might lead to a lack of diversity in hiring, including too much recycling of old actors.

Finally, Joshua Glick , visiting associate professor of film and electronic arts at Bard College in Annandale-on-Hudson, New York, laments the potential loss of the social aspects of a film industry run by AI: “What it means to follow a project as it's unfolding, not just to watch a finished film or stream a TV show, is important. Our knowledge of the creative labour and these human dimensions of it are part of the enjoyment that spectators derive from the films and part of what makes the film and television industry distinct. It needs to do its business in public.”
