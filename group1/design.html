Production Design

Many of the most promising—and least controversial—uses of generative AI in film making are in the area of production design. AI can be used (with varying degrees of naturalness) to generate both still and moving images, as well as in the creation of concept art, animation, and visual effects. In 1991, James Cameron's sci-fi epic "Terminator 2: Judgment Day" (1991) showcased groundbreaking CGI (computer-generated imagery) effects that pushed the boundaries of what was possible with computer graphics at the time.

AI algorithms were instrumental in simulating the fluid movements of the iconic liquid metal T-1000 Terminator, laying the groundwork for future advancements in CGI technology. The use of generative AI in film began with Peter Jackson's epic fantasy saga based on J.R.R. Tolkien's "The Lord of the Rings" Trilogy (2001-2003). For these films, Weta Digital developed Massive to create crowd scenes for battles that consisted of different kinds of “agents” with characteristic actions. When two of these AI agents met in battle, the outcome was not predictable. Massive was also used to create crowd simulations for the films “Avatar” (2009) and “The Avengers” (2012).

Uses

One use of generative AI in production design is to enhance graphics created through more traditional means. In "The Avengers," production designers used CGI and AI (Masquerade) tools in tandem to give Thanos recognizable emotions that would resonate with the audience. In the 2023 Oscar winner "Everything Everywhere All at Once," visual effects editor Evan Halleck used Runway to automate tasks like rotoscoping that are time-consuming and imprecise when done frame-by-frame by hand. AI also allows production designers to create and modify virtual environments quickly and in real-time, which can eliminate the need for expensive physical sets and time-consuming changes to those sets. In turn, by reducing the time and costs associated with creating visual effects that can be automated, the use of AI gives artists the space to be more creative in other directions.

Generative AI can also be used to create graphics like concept art and 3D models. AI models like Midjourney , DALL-E, and Stable Diffusion can be used to create still images for use in backgrounds and landscapes, and although the images created by these models still suffer from the uncanny valley effect , they have been undergoing rapid improvement in recent years.

The most controversial use of AI in production design is the use of programs like Runway and Pika Labs to create moving images from a text prompt or a reference image. However, while this technology is improving quickly, it currently still struggles to present physics and anatomy in natural ways. Less comprehensive uses of AI in the creation of moving images include the use of AI to animate still images to make a character nod their head or appear to be talking and the use of machine learning to track movement of real objects to use in the creation of naturalistic motion effects. AI can also be used to create hybrids of live-action and AI-generated content: this is concerning for its potential use in creating deepfakes, but is hard to do convincingly with current technology.

Concerns

The increasing use of generative AI in production design has led to fears about job security for artists and crew members, and especially for those in entry-level positions that would be assigned the time-consuming hand-work that AI can automate. However, since there is also a learning curve associated with the use of AI tools, it is not clear that benefits to efficiency (and subsequent job losses) will be seen as quickly as they are anticipated.

There are also concerns that the ease of creating content using AI will result in a decrease in the quality of work produced by the entertainment industry through the generation of visuals that lack artistic merit and humanity and that create cognitive dissonance (e.g., the uncanny valley). Finally, as in other areas of filmmaking, there are ethical concerns about the source of the data being used to train the AI software and whether Ai-generated derivative works can be copyright protected.

Response

Indian filmmaker Shekhar Kapur is hopeful that the use of AI will democratize the process of filmmaking by making it quicker, easier, and cheaper: "If everyone has access to the same tools, organisational hierarchies will break and everyone will have the power to tell a story."
