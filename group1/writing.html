Writing

Much of the current conversation about the use of generative AI in filmmaking was spurred by the 2023 WGA (Writer's Guild of America) strike. The two main issues in this labor dispute with the AMPTP (Alliance of Motion Picture and Television Producers)--residuals from streaming media and the role of generative AI tools like ChatGPT in scriptwriting—both highlighted ways that new technologies are impacting writers in the film industry.

Uses

Currently AI tools like Scriptbook can be used to create full scripts in specific genres or on specific topics within minutes, including dialogue, character arcs, and scene directions. Other tools, like StoryFit, can be used to fine-tune scripts to produce movies that will appeal to particular audiences. Writers can also use AI-generated text as sources of inspiration to enhance their own creativity

Concerns

That said, there is concern that use of AI-generated writing will lead to job losses for scriptwriters, accompanied by an increase in formulaic content in the film industry, since AI cannot achieve the same level of creativity and emotional depth that human writers can. Beyond this, there are ethical concerns with the way that AI text generators are trained (Where does the data come from? What biases does it contain? Was permission granted to use the data to train an AI system? Does it violate data privacy restrictions?) and whether AI-generated works can themselves be copyrighted.

Response

Part of the motivation for the WGA strike was to “regulate use of material produced using artificial intelligence or similar technologies.” Specifically, representatives of the writers wanted assurances that AI would not be used for writing or rewriting literary material, that they would not be asked to adapt or rewrite AI-generated source material, and that their own work would not be used to train AI technologies. The writers and studios reached a tentative agreement on these issues in late September 2023.

According to Deloitte's 18th annual Digital Media Trends survey, 70% of US consumers surveyed would rather watch a TV show or movie written by a human than by generative AI.

Indian director Shekhar Kapur experimented with ChatGPT when writing the sequel to his 1983 film Masoom and “was amazed at "how intuitively AI understood the moral conflict in the plot", but ultimately decided that humans were better able than AI to understand the nuances of human interactions: AI “cannot create mystery, feel fear or love.” In a 2023 profile of the Academy Award-nominated American screenwriter Scott Frank, New Yorker staff writer Patrick Radden Keefe expressed similar sentiments: “AI can certainly assemble a recombinant screenplay by drawing on the collected works of people like Scott Frank, and the result may even be schematically sound—but getting an audience to really care about what happens to fictional characters requires a different sort of magic.”
